# The AI Visibility Index rubric — what we measure, and what your score means

*Customer-facing rubric overview. Marty's voice. From the report's "show your work" link.*

---

## What this rubric measures, in one paragraph

When your buyer asks ChatGPT, Claude, or Perplexity for a recommendation in your category, two things determine whether you get named: how well your business is *built* to be found by AI, and how often AI is *actually* finding you right now. The rubric measures both. The first half is called your **Readiness score**. The second half is called your **Visibility score**. Together they roll up into a single number on a 0–100 scale — your **AI Visibility Index**.

The whole methodology is published. Every score traces to observable evidence. Every band cites the research it's based on. If you want to dig into the technical version with all the citations, that's at [link to /methodology/rubric]. This page is the plain-English version.

## The two sides of the score

### Readiness — *are you built to be found?*

This is what we look at on your own website and on the third-party platforms where AI engines collect their information. We measure five things:

- **Whether your business is one clear entity** across the web. AI engines need to know that the *you* on your website, on LinkedIn, on your Google business profile, and in directories are all the same business. Contradictions make you fuzzy. Consistency makes you findable.
- **Whether third parties vouch for you.** AI engines disproportionately cite Reddit, Wikipedia, YouTube, LinkedIn, and industry analyst sites — not your own website. We check whether you show up there, and whether other people are talking about you there.
- **Whether your content is easy for AI to read.** Clean structure, clear headings, schema markup, and metadata that describes what you do (not just your name). We also check for one anti-signal: keyword stuffing, which actually *hurts* visibility.
- **Whether your content is different from everyone else's.** This is the most important dimension. AI engines specifically filter for sources that add something new to the conversation. We look for your distinctive data, signature methodology, named case studies, or original perspective — and we check whether they're surfaced in a place AI can extract them.
- **Whether you're on the platforms your buyer's AI tools actually use.** ChatGPT leans on Wikipedia, Forbes, established news. Claude leans on academic and analyst sources. Perplexity leans on Reddit, YouTube, and review sites. Being on the wrong platform is the same as being invisible.

You get a 0-to-100 score for Readiness based on these five.

### Visibility — *are you actually being found?*

This is what we measure live, by asking ChatGPT, Claude, and Perplexity the same questions your buyers ask. We send four queries to each engine and capture exactly what each one said. Then we count:

- **Presence** — how often you were mentioned at all.
- **Citation** — how often you were mentioned with a working link back to your site.
- **Share of voice** — when AI named businesses in your category, what fraction of those mentions were yours versus your competitors'.
- **Prominence** — where in the AI's answer you appeared. First mention counts more than buried-at-the-bottom mention.

You get a 0-to-100 score for Visibility based on those four.

### Your composite — the AI Visibility Index

The composite is a weighted blend: 40% Readiness, 60% Visibility. Readiness is the leading indicator. Visibility is what your buyers actually experience today. The 60/40 weighting reflects that distinction — if you're already visible, you're already winning; if you're not visible yet, what you do to improve Readiness is what will eventually move Visibility.

Your composite is a number between 0 and 100. That number falls into one of four categories.

## The four categories

### Invisible *(score below 25)*

AI engines do not surface your business when buyers ask. You may not even appear if your buyer types your business name directly. When AI is asked to recommend someone in your category, it lists your competitors and not you. Search results in AI tools may not include your website's URL even though it exists.

**What this usually means.** Either you're a very new business that AI hasn't picked up yet, or the public-facing structure of your business is confusing AI's pattern-matching. Sometimes both. If you don't have an active LinkedIn or Google business profile, if your website is short on content, or if competitors have flooded the platforms AI cites from — any of these can produce this score.

**What this typically isn't.** It is not a sign that your business is bad, or that AI tools "don't like you." It is a sign that AI tools don't know you exist yet. That's a solvable problem with specific moves.

**The honest read.** If you're at this level and you want to be visible in AI within the next six to twelve months, you'll need to do some structural work — clarifying your entity across the web, publishing content that has something distinctive in it, and earning a small handful of third-party mentions on the right platforms. None of those moves are mysterious. They take focused attention, not a budget.

---

### Emerging *(score between 25 and 55)*

AI engines mention you sometimes. Not consistently, not for every relevant query, but enough that the right buyer with the right question will sometimes find their way to you. You're partway there. You probably have one or two strong signals working in your favor — a solid LinkedIn presence, maybe an active Google business profile, possibly some industry mentions — but other signals are missing or inconsistent.

**What this usually means.** Most established small businesses with a working website land somewhere in this range. The base infrastructure exists. What's missing is usually one of two things: either your distinctive content isn't surfaced where AI can find it (it might be in your head, or in a slide deck, or buried on page four of your website instead of on the homepage), or you're missing presence on a platform that's important for your category's AI engine of choice.

**What this typically isn't.** A score in this range does not mean you have to start over. Most of the foundational work is already done. What you're missing is targeted — and small, well-chosen moves often produce outsized gains here. The research shows that businesses ranked in the middle of search results get the largest measurable boosts from publishing differentiated content. The gains compound.

**The honest read.** This is the most actionable category. If you're here, two or three focused moves over a quarter — done thoughtfully, not as a checklist — will usually move your score by 10 to 20 points. The challenge is choosing the right two or three moves for your specific business. That's most of what the audit's recommendations are for.

---

### Discoverable *(score between 55 and 80)*

AI engines find you reliably when your category is mentioned. Buyers searching for the kind of thing you sell are likely to hear your name. Competitors come up too — you're not alone in the AI's recommendations — but you're in the conversation. Your content is being cited, your site is being linked to, and your reputation signals are surfacing.

**What this usually means.** You've done structural work that's paying off. Your entity is clear. You have third-party corroboration. Your distinctive content exists and is at least partly findable. You're on at least some of the right platforms. The pieces are mostly in place.

**What this typically isn't.** This is not a position you can take for granted. AI engines change their preferences every few months. Competitors below you can move quickly with the right work. And — this is the surprising part — many of the tactics that worked to get you here may actually *hurt* you now. The research is specific: businesses at this level can lose visibility from "more comprehensive content" or "more authoritative tone" because comprehensive overlaps with what's already out there, and AI filters for non-redundancy.

**The honest read.** The work at this level is different from the work at lower levels. It's about making sure your differentiated edge is clear, deepening your presence on the platforms where you already have a foothold, and refusing the generic-content advice you'll often hear from marketing vendors. The moves are smaller and more strategic. The wins are in defending and deepening, not in adding more.

---

### Agent-Ready *(score above 80)*

AI engines treat you as a primary source. When buyers ask for recommendations in your category, you are reliably named — often first, often with a direct citation. Your site shows up in the engine's reference list. Your reputation surfaces with positive sentiment. Your differentiated content is being quoted back in answers AI gives to other businesses asking related questions.

**What this usually means.** You've built something real. Your entity is unambiguous. Your distinctive contribution is recognized and surfaced. You're present where it matters. Competitors are aware of you, and so are AI engines.

**What this typically isn't.** This is not a permanent state. AI engines drift. Sourcing patterns change. New competitors enter. A position at this level is something you maintain, not something you achieve and then stop tending. But the maintenance work is much smaller than the work that got you here — and it's mostly about staying current with where your buyers' attention actually lives.

**The honest read.** If you're at this level, your AI visibility is no longer a marketing problem. It's a category-leadership problem. The right moves now are about expanding what makes you distinctive, contributing to the conversations on the platforms that matter, and tracking what your competitors are doing differently so you don't get displaced. The score won't drop overnight. It also won't stay there by itself.

## How the score relates to what we measure

The four categories are useful for understanding where you stand. But your *audit report* gives you more than the category. It gives you:

- The score for each of the five Readiness dimensions
- The score for each of the four Visibility sub-metrics
- The exact engine responses we captured when we asked your buyer's questions to ChatGPT, Claude, and Perplexity
- A short list of differentiation candidates we observed on your website (or, if we didn't observe any, the questions to ask yourself to find them)
- Specific recommendations ranked by impact-per-hour

The category tells you *where you are.* The dimensions tell you *why.* The recommendations tell you *what to do.* And the methodology page tells you *how we know.*

---

## A note on what a single score does and doesn't tell you

A composite number compresses a lot of information into one digit. That makes it useful for tracking progress over time, and for having a clean conversation about where your business stands. It also flattens nuance.

A score of 47 and a score of 52 are not meaningfully different. They reflect different combinations of strengths and weaknesses that happen to roll up to roughly the same place. What's actionable isn't the composite number — it's the pattern of dimension scores underneath it.

That's why the audit report doesn't lead with the composite. It leads with your differentiation profile — what you have that nobody else has — and only then shows you the structural picture. The score is a way to know whether the work is moving the needle. The dimensions are what tell you which work to do.

If you find yourself focused mostly on the composite number, that's a signal to spend more time on the differentiation profile. That's where the leverage actually lives.
