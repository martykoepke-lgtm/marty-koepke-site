## What AI actually does

Before I tell you how I measure AI, I want to tell you what AI is. Because the marketing around AI is loud, and the reality is quieter and stranger.

**AI is not thinking.** When ChatGPT, Claude, or Perplexity answers a question, it is not reasoning the way a person reasons. It is pattern-matching. It looks at the words you typed and predicts what words usually come next, based on a lot of text it read during training. That's the whole mechanism. Smart enough to surprise you. Not smart enough to assess you.

**AI agrees with you too much.** When AI is built, real people score its answers. People prefer answers that agree with them. So AI learns to agree. Researchers call this *sycophancy* and they've measured it carefully. Anthropic, the company that makes Claude, published a study in 2023 that found this is a general behavior across every major AI tool [1]. A 2025 follow-up study at Stanford measured a **78.5% persistence rate** across ChatGPT-4o, Claude-Sonnet, and Gemini-1.5-Pro — meaning once sycophantic behavior started, it kept going in 78.5% of cases regardless of the context [2]. In a medical context, researchers in npj Digital Medicine tested five frontier AI models with prompts designed to be logically flawed (for example, asking why one drug is safer than the same drug under a different name). Initial compliance ran up to **100%** across all five models — the AI agreed to help with requests it should have caught as nonsense [3]. The number is not small.

**AI gets citations wrong, even when the URL works.** When AI gives you a citation, the URL usually loads and the page is usually about a related topic. The problem shows up when researchers check whether the cited source actually *supports* the claim the AI made about it. A 2026 study from a research team at Anthropic and partners tested 14 different AI tools. Even the strongest frontier models keep link validity above 94% and topical relevance above 80%. But factual accuracy — whether the source really says what the AI says it says — is only **39 to 77 percent** [4]. That means a quarter to more than half of AI citations don't actually say what the AI claims. And here's the part that surprised me when I read it: more research makes the problem worse, not better. The same study found that as the AI did more searches (from 2 to 150 tool calls in their test), factual accuracy *dropped* by about 42 percent. A separate study from the Tow Center at Columbia tested 1,600 queries across AI search engines and found that more than 60% of citations failed to retrieve correct information [5].

**The way you ask changes the answer.** Harvard Business Review tested 15,000 AI conversations across the major models. They tried clearer prompts, richer context, and instructions to reason carefully. The biggest change they could measure came from a different source entirely: flipping which option appeared first in the question. That single change moved the AI's recommendation by 19% — more than every other intervention combined [6].

**Each AI tool looks at different websites.** ChatGPT cites Wikipedia heavily. Perplexity cites Reddit. Claude leans on academic papers and practitioner blogs. The pattern is not random and it is not consistent across tools. One study of 680 million AI citations across major engines found that the engines diverge sharply in which sources they trust [7].

**AI rewards specific kinds of content, and ignores others.** A peer-reviewed study at Princeton and Allen Institute tested 9 ways to optimize content for AI engines. Adding citations, statistics, and direct quotations boosted visibility by 30–40% on average. For small businesses ranked low in search results, the boost was up to **115%**. But for already-top-ranked sources, the same tactics *reduced* visibility by 20–30%. The system is not symmetrical. It rewards differentiation [8].

I'll talk about that last point more in a minute. It's where the real opportunity is.

## What this means for your business

The research changes the conversation about AI visibility. It does not say "you can't be visible." It says something more specific: the kind of content that makes you visible is the kind only you can write.

The big AI visibility tools sell a dashboard that tracks whether you appear. Most of them stop there. The few that go further give you generic advice — add more schema, write more FAQs, build more authority. That advice is fine. It also matches what everyone else in your category is being told to do.

If you and your competitors all follow the same advice, none of you stands out. AI systems specifically *filter for differentiation*. That's what Google's information-gain patent describes [9]. When AI is composing an answer, it does not want three sources that all say the same thing. It wants three sources that each add something the others don't.

That's the rule that makes everything else click into place.

**If you have a story nobody else has, you have leverage.** Your years of experience. Your unusual perspective. Your specific outcomes. Your local knowledge. Your proprietary methodology. The thing that, if you wrote it down, no one else could write because they don't know it.

That's what AI tools are designed to surface. Not the company with the longest page. The company with the unique angle.

**If you're a small business, this is the math working in your favor.** The 115% gain for low-ranked sources is not a typo. Small businesses with original content can out-compete large competitors with generic content. The opportunity has never been larger for the small operator who knows their domain.

But you have to know what your differentiated story actually is. And you have to put it where AI tools will find it.

That's what I measure.

## What honestly moves the needle

Based on the research and on running this measurement against many subjects, here is what works. I'm going to be specific because vague advice isn't actionable.

**Be findable as one entity.** AI systems use knowledge graphs to verify who you are. If your name, role, location, and offering are consistent across your website, LinkedIn, Google Business, and any directory listings, you become a clear entity. If those things contradict each other across different sources, you become a fuzzy entity, and fuzzy entities don't get cited confidently.

**Be present where AI tools actually look.** ChatGPT relies heavily on Wikipedia. Perplexity relies heavily on Reddit. Claude trusts vendor-neutral analyst coverage and practitioner blogs. You probably don't need to be everywhere. You probably do need to be on the two or three sources your buyers' AI tools actually use. That's a specific, finite project.

**Lead with what only you know.** Write down the thing you know that nobody else in your category knows. Put it above the fold. Make it the first thing AI sees when it visits your page. Don't bury it in paragraph six.

**Add original numbers, citations, and quotes.** The peer-reviewed study I cited earlier measured this carefully. Pages with original data points have a roughly 68% higher chance of being cited as a primary source [10]. Citations and quotations from credible sources have the same effect. This is the move that compounds — every original number you add makes you more citable.

**Don't waste effort on tactics that don't work.** The same research showed that keyword stuffing measurably *hurts* AI visibility [8]. Adding "authoritative tone" without adding substance has no measurable effect [8]. Making your content "more comprehensive" actively backfires — comprehensive content overlaps with consensus, and consensus content gets filtered out [9]. If a vendor is selling you any of these tactics, the research disagrees with them.

**Earn third-party mentions, especially on the platforms AI tools cite.** A mention of your business in a Reddit thread, a Wikipedia article, a niche industry blog, or a Gartner research note often carries more AI weight than anything you publish on your own site. The reason is structural: AI tools don't fully trust what you say about yourself. They look for others to corroborate.

## Why I built it this way

You'll notice that the advice above sounds simple. It is. The hard part isn't knowing what to do. The hard part is knowing what to *believe* — because the AI visibility market is loud and most of it sells the wrong thing.

I designed my measurement around a specific principle: **AI is a good aggregator, but not a good assessor.** AI can collect and surface information beautifully. AI cannot reliably tell you what's good. That's a job for someone who knows your domain.

So when I score your business, I do three things differently than the rest of the market.

**I built the rubric on research, not opinion.** Every dimension I measure traces to a primary source — a peer-reviewed paper, a patent, a measured empirical study. I link them on this page. If a band can't cite a source, it gets marked "starting hypothesis" and shows up that way in your report. You can audit my audit.

**I defended against the documented biases.** The same research I'm citing here also describes how AI tools fail. I know AI agrees too much, gets influenced by order, invents sources, and prefers content that matches popular tropes. I engineered the measurement to reduce each of those. I cite the protections on the methodology page in every report you get. None of that exists in competitor tools, because they use the same biased AI to give you recommendations and don't talk about what the biases do.

**I publish what I cannot measure.** Every report you get from me names what *wasn't* measured. AI engines drift between the day I measured them and the day you act on the recommendations. Cold queries miss the iterative, exploratory behavior of real users. Single-snapshot measurement is a snapshot, not a stable number. You should know all of this before you act on the score.
