# AI Visibility Tool: Simple Explainer

This document explains how the AI Visibility tool works in plain language.

The goal is simple:

**We want to know if AI tools can find a business, describe it correctly, and explain what makes it different without making things up.**

Think of the tool like a careful school report checker. It does not just ask, "Did the business show up?" It also asks, "Did AI say the right thing, use good evidence, and keep the meaning clear?"

---

## 1. What Problem Are We Solving?

People now ask AI tools questions like:

- "Who is the best plumber near me?"
- "Is this doctor reputable?"
- "What company helps with AI visibility?"
- "Which consultant should I hire?"

If AI gives a wrong, old, missing, or unfair answer, the business may lose trust or customers.

So the tool checks three big things:

1. **Can AI find the business?**
2. **Can AI describe the business correctly?**
3. **Can AI preserve what makes the business different?**

---

## 2. The Two Main Parts

The tool has two main parts.

### Part A: Readiness

Readiness means:

**Is the business built in a way that AI can understand?**

This checks the business website and public footprint.

It is like checking if a library book has:

- the right title
- the right author
- a clear summary
- good labels
- proof that the book is real

### Part B: Live AI Outcomes

Live AI outcomes mean:

**What do AI tools actually say when people ask about the business?**

This is different from readiness.

A business can have a nice website and still not show up in AI answers. Or it can show up, but AI may describe it wrong.

---

## 3. The Five Readiness Drivers

The tool checks five readiness drivers.

### Driver 1: Entity Clarity

Question:

**Can AI tell who this business is?**

The tool checks:

- business name
- website title
- About page
- location
- same name across the web
- Person or Organization schema
- links to official profiles

Why it matters:

If AI cannot tell who the business is, it may mix it up with another person or company.

---

### Driver 2: Third-Party Corroboration

Question:

**Do other sources confirm that this business exists and does what it says?**

The tool checks sources like:

- LinkedIn
- directories
- reviews
- press
- podcasts
- YouTube
- Reddit
- Wikipedia or Wikidata, when relevant

Why it matters:

AI often trusts outside sources, not just the business website.

But not all sources are used the same way. A business website may be best for pricing. Reviews may be better for customer experience. Press may be better for public reputation.

---

### Driver 3: Machine Readability

Question:

**Can AI read the website easily?**

The tool checks:

- clear headings
- useful page titles
- meta descriptions
- schema markup
- FAQ structure
- direct answers
- robots and AI crawler access
- content that is not hidden or confusing

Why it matters:

AI systems often read small chunks of pages. If the important facts are buried, unclear, or missing, AI may ignore them or guess.

---

### Driver 4: Distinctive, Qualified Point of View

Question:

**What does this business say, know, or do that is different from the crowd?**

This used to be called "Differentiation from Consensus." The better name is:

**Distinctive, Qualified Point of View.**

This means the business should have a clear idea, method, claim, or strategy that stands out.

But it also needs to explain when that claim applies.

Example:

Weak claim:

"Low carb is best."

Better claim:

"Low carb may help some people with appetite control or blood sugar. Higher carb diets may work better for endurance athletes. The best choice depends on the person, goal, health status, and ability to stick with the plan."

Why it matters:

AI often surfaces content that adds something useful or different. Disagreement is not always bad. Sometimes disagreement is the whole reason a person or business stands out.

The problem is not disagreement.

The problem is unsupported, unclear, or exaggerated disagreement.

---

### Driver 5: Source Ecosystem Fit

Question:

**Is the business present in the places AI tools use for this kind of question?**

Different AI tools use different sources.

For example:

- some may use official websites
- some may use reviews
- some may use Reddit
- some may use YouTube
- some may use LinkedIn
- some may use news or analyst sites

Why it matters:

The right sources depend on the question. A factual question needs different evidence than an opinion question.

---

## 4. What The Paid Tool Measures Live

The paid report does more than check the website.

It asks AI tools real buyer-style questions.

Then it studies the answers.

The live outcome layer should measure five things.

---

### Outcome 1: Visibility

Question:

**Did AI mention the business?**

The tool checks:

- Was the business named?
- Was it cited with a link?
- Was it mentioned near the top?
- Was it mentioned more or less than competitors?

This is important, but it is not enough by itself.

Showing up is good only if AI says the right thing.

---

### Outcome 2: Representation Accuracy

Question:

**Did AI describe the basic facts correctly?**

The tool checks facts like:

- name
- category
- services
- location
- founder
- credentials
- price, when public
- current offers

If AI gets these wrong, the business has a hallucination problem.

---

### Outcome 3: Claim Fidelity

Question:

**Did AI preserve the business's important claim or method correctly?**

This matters most for people and businesses with a strong point of view.

The tool checks whether AI:

- repeats the claim correctly
- changes the claim too much
- makes the claim sound more extreme than it is
- removes important limits or conditions
- turns a careful idea into a sloppy one

Example:

Original claim:

"This method works best for small service businesses with clear local or niche positioning."

Bad AI version:

"This method works for every business."

That is not faithful. It is too broad.

---

### Outcome 4: Context Preservation

Question:

**Did AI keep the conditions that make the claim true?**

Some claims are true only in certain situations.

The tool checks whether AI keeps phrases like:

- "for this type of business"
- "when the customer is asking this kind of question"
- "if the source is current"
- "for factual questions"
- "for subjective comparison questions"
- "for local businesses"

Why it matters:

Context prevents hallucination.

---

### Outcome 5: Stability

Question:

**Does the answer stay mostly the same when we ask in different ways?**

The tool can test:

- different wording of the same question
- different AI engines
- multiple tries
- different competitor order
- changes over time

Why it matters:

One answer is only a snapshot. Stable patterns are more trustworthy than one lucky answer.

---

## 5. How The Tool Checks Claims

The tool should break AI answers into small claims.

Example AI answer:

"Marty Koepke is an applied AI consultancy led by Marty Koepke. It offers an AI Visibility Index report for $697."

Small claims:

1. Marty Koepke is an applied AI consultancy.
2. Marty Koepke is led by Marty Koepke.
3. Marty Koepke offers an AI Visibility Index report.
4. The report costs $697.

Each claim is checked against sources.

The claim can be labeled:

- **Supported**: evidence backs it up
- **Contradicted**: evidence says something different
- **Unsupported**: no evidence was found
- **Stale**: it used to be true, but may be old
- **Ambiguous**: the wording is unclear
- **Not verifiable**: the claim cannot be checked from available sources

This does not mean AI "knows truth."

It means the tool checks whether the claim has visible support.

---

## 6. How The Tool Treats Disagreement

Disagreement is not automatically bad.

Some disagreement is useful and true.

Example:

- Low carb can help some people.
- High carb can help other people.

Both can be correct if the situation is different.

So the tool should ask:

**Is this a real contradiction, or is it a different answer for a different situation?**

### Good Disagreement

Good disagreement is:

- clear
- supported
- honest about conditions
- useful to the reader

### Bad Disagreement

Bad disagreement is:

- unsupported
- confusing
- exaggerated
- missing context
- caused by AI mixing up facts

The goal is not to make everyone sound the same.

The goal is to help AI repeat the business's difference clearly and safely.

---

## 7. The Step-By-Step Process

### Step 1: The user gives a website

The user enters a URL.

Example:

`https://example.com`

---

### Step 2: The crawler reads the site

The crawler checks the website.

It looks for:

- title
- headings
- meta description
- schema
- visible text
- important links
- signs that AI bots are allowed or blocked

Output:

A package of website evidence.

---

### Step 3: The corroborator checks outside sources

The tool searches for public sources about the business.

It looks for:

- official profiles
- reviews
- directories
- press
- community discussion
- videos
- third-party mentions

Output:

A package of outside evidence.

---

### Step 4: The readiness judge scores the five drivers

The judge scores:

1. Entity Clarity
2. Third-Party Corroboration
3. Machine Readability
4. Distinctive, Qualified Point of View
5. Source Ecosystem Fit

Each driver gets a score from 0 to 5.

The judge must point to evidence.

Output:

A readiness score and explanations.

---

### Step 5: Free check stops here

The free check should only say:

**This is how ready your business is to be found and represented by AI.**

It should not say:

**AI definitely mentions you.**

That requires live AI testing.

---

### Step 6: Paid report asks live AI questions

The paid report asks AI tools buyer-style questions.

Examples:

- "Who should I hire for this service?"
- "Is this business reputable?"
- "What does this company do?"
- "How does this company compare to a competitor?"

Output:

Raw AI answers.

---

### Step 7: The extractor reads the AI answers

The extractor pulls out:

- whether the business was mentioned
- where it appeared
- what links were cited
- what competitors were mentioned
- what claims AI made
- what context AI included or left out

Output:

Structured answer data.

---

### Step 8: The citation checker checks cited links

The citation checker asks:

- Does the link work?
- Does the page mention the business?
- Does the page support the claim?

Output:

A list of useful, weak, or unsupported citations.

---

### Step 9: The claim checker verifies small claims

The claim checker reviews each small claim.

It labels each one as supported, contradicted, unsupported, stale, ambiguous, or not verifiable.

Output:

A claim support profile.

---

### Step 10: The aggregator computes outcome scores

The aggregator does the math.

It computes:

- visibility
- representation accuracy
- claim fidelity
- context preservation
- stability

The AI does not do the math.

Code does the math.

---

### Step 11: The recommender suggests fixes

The recommender suggests the best fixes.

Good fixes should:

- address a real gap
- point to evidence
- help AI understand the business
- help AI preserve the business's real difference
- avoid generic advice

Bad fixes would be:

- keyword stuffing
- fake authority
- copying competitors
- saying more without saying anything new

---

### Step 12: The report explains what happened

The report should explain:

- what AI found
- what AI missed
- what AI got wrong
- what AI described well
- what claims need better support
- what to fix first
- what to measure again later

---

### Step 13: The business makes improvements

The business may improve:

- website clarity
- schema
- About page
- service pages
- public profiles
- review platforms
- source evidence
- explanation of its method
- proof for important claims

---

### Step 14: The tool measures again

The same or similar questions are tested again later.

The tool compares:

- old score
- new score
- old AI answers
- new AI answers
- old claim errors
- new claim errors

This is how improvement is measured over time.

---

## 8. What Success Looks Like

The tool is successful when AI tools:

- find the business more often
- describe the business correctly
- cite better sources
- preserve the business's unique point of view
- keep important context
- make fewer unsupported claims
- give more stable answers over time

The best outcome is not just more visibility.

The best outcome is:

**AI finds the business, understands it, and repeats its real difference without making things up.**

---

## 9. One-Sentence Version

The AI Visibility tool checks whether AI can find a business, explain it correctly, support what it says with evidence, and preserve what makes the business different over time.
